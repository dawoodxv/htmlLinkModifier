<h2 class="page-header header-two"><span style="color: #085394;">Unit Overview</span></h2>
<div>
<p>The profession of social work has played an oppressive role when it comes to Indigenous People. In addition, social work research practice with Indigenous people has historically operated from a colonial perspective, and a Western positivist paradigm. However today, Indigenous social workers and other Indigenous scholars are providing missing perspectives, which help to inform critical reflection of this legacy of colonization in social work research and practice. Anti-oppressive and Indigenous researchers who are revitalizing ongoing research approaches and formulating new Indigenous research methodologies are also invigorating contemporary social work research as a whole.</p>
<p>This unit will aid you in considering the different ways that you as future social work researchers may engage in anti-oppressive practice and work to advance healing, and combat racism and colonialism, which still oppresses Indigenous people. This unit will also help you understand what constitutes culturally safe research practices, the principles of conducting ethical research in Indigenous contexts, and the difference between Indigenous ways of knowing and Western worldview and research.</p>
</div>

</ol>
<h2 class="page-header header-two"><span style="color: #085394;">Learning Objectives</span></h2>
<h3>By the end of this unit you should be able to:</h3>
<ul class="checklist">
<li>Students will understand the difference between Indigenous ways of knowing and western worldviews and research.</li>
<li>Students will understand ethical principles related to research with Indigenous communities.</li>
<li>Students will understand what constitutes culturally safe research practices.</li>
</ul>
